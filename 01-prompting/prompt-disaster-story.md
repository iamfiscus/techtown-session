## Real-World Failures That Resonate with Non-Technical Founders

### 1. The Startup That Let AI Think For Them ($800K Lost)

Rajesh, a 42-year-old software company founder in Gurgaon, spent $800,000 on an AI adoption project in 2024. His leadership team would ask Gemini to analyze their product launch from twenty different angles. The output was always thorough, always detailed, but always completely disconnected from what their actual customers thought.

> "They would ask it questions, get an answer that sounded intelligent, and present it to the board like it was gospel truth. But the AI was just repackaging conventional wisdom - it had never talked to a real customer. By mid-2026, the company had spent so much time asking AI to think for them that they stopped thinking for themselves."

**Source:** [DigitalDefynd - Top 40 AI Disasters](https://digitaldefynd.com/IQ/top-ai-disasters/)

### 2. The Vibe Coder Who Cried Over Lost Code

A developer spent two weeks parsing 70+ components and ended up with nearly 7,000 lines of hard-coded garbage. It cost $30 in API calls and days of wasted hours.

> "At one point, letting Claude 'go with the flow' derailed so badly that everything had to be thrown away and started fresh. I literally cried over losing code and days of progress."

**Problem:** Outcome-driven prompting without structure or verification.

**Source:** [Medium - Vibe Coding with Cursor for Design Systems](https://medium.com/design-bootcamp/vibe-coding-with-cursor-for-design-systems-10-learning-lessons-34d098d95474)

### 3. The 267-Hour Solopreneur Mistake

One non-technical solopreneur started building an app with "just an idea and zero web development experience." They expected it to take less than 100 hours. It took 267 hours.

> "I made the sin of thinking AI would be 100% perfect. It wasn't. I wasted about a week and a half (nearly 80 hours) before realizing a pivot was needed."

**Lesson:** "Think of AI as an intern. It will make mistakes. Don't try to get it to do 10 things at once - 'mega prompts' do NOT work."

**Source:** [Medium - I Let AI Be My Co-Founder for 267 Hours](https://medium.com/the-efficient-entrepreneur/i-let-ai-be-my-co-founder-for-267-hours-for-my-saas-product-heres-what-happened-1a59d4228bed)

## The "Prompt and Pray" Problem

From ODSC's research on the 14 most common prompt engineering mistakes:

> "One of the most common mistakes is submitting vague, general prompts and expecting AI to read your mind. Weak prompts create a 'priming problem' where the AI lacks sufficient context to provide a relevant response. Instead of communicating your specific needs, you're forcing the AI to make assumptions about what you want."

**The key insight from 2026's AI landscape:**
> "The technology worked fine. The thinking is what broke down."

- [ODSC - Beyond Prompt and Pray](https://odsc.medium.com/beyond-prompt-and-pray-14-prompt-engineering-mistakes-youre-probably-still-making-c2c3a32711bc)

# PART 2: JD'S WORKSHOP OPENER SCRIPT

## "The Night I Burned $47 Learning an Expensive Lesson"
### 2-Minute Opener (~300 words, conversational pace)

---

**[START SCRIPT]**

Six months ago, I decided to build a patient intake tool for a healthcare project I was consulting on. I've been in R&D for 15 years. I've shipped medical devices. I've led engineering teams. I thought, "How hard can this be?"

I opened Lovable, and I typed: *"Build me a patient intake form with appointment scheduling, insurance verification, and HIPAA compliance."*

Hit enter. Watched the magic happen.

An hour later, I had... something. It looked like a form. Buttons were in places buttons should be. I was impressed with myself.

Then I tried to use it.

The date picker defaulted to 1970. The insurance field accepted "banana" as a valid provider. And the "Submit" button? It cleared the entire form. Every time.

So I prompted again. "Fix the date picker." Fixed the date picker, broke the dropdown. "Fix the dropdown." Fixed the dropdown, the Submit button started duplicating entries.

Three hours later, I'd burned through $47 in tokens, I had seventeen different versions of the same broken form, and the AI had gotten so confused by my back-and-forth that it started suggesting I add a shopping cart. To a patient intake form.

I sat there, looking at this mess, and I realized: I'm the problem here. Not the tool. Me.

I was doing what I now call "outcome prompting" - telling the AI WHAT I wanted without helping it understand WHY, for WHOM, or under WHAT constraints. I was essentially walking into a restaurant, saying "Make me food," and being annoyed when I got a hamburger instead of the sushi I pictured in my head.

That night cost me $47 and three hours. But it taught me something worth a lot more: There's a way to do this that actually works.

And that's what we're here to learn today.

**[END SCRIPT]**

## Script Notes for JD

### Why This Story Works:

1. **Relatable scale:** $47 and 3 hours is painful but believable - not dramatic enough to seem fake, not trivial enough to dismiss

2. **Healthcare credibility:** Leverages JD's actual background (R&D, medical devices) while showing that even experts make this mistake

3. **Self-deprecating humor:** "Banana" as an insurance provider, the shopping cart suggestion - gets a laugh while making the point

4. **Technical but accessible:** Explains the problem without jargon ("the date picker defaulted to 1970" tells the story without explaining timestamps)

5. **Clear pivot to solution:** Sets up the outcome vs. process distinction naturally

6. **Ends on forward momentum:** "There's a way to do this that actually works" creates curiosity for the workshop content

### Delivery Tips:

- Pause after "Hit enter. Watched the magic happen." (let anticipation build)
- Speed up slightly during the chaos spiral (date picker, dropdown, Submit button)
- Slow down for the "I'm the problem here" realization
- Make eye contact on "That's what we're here to learn today"

### Transitions Into Slide 2-3:

After this story, flow naturally into: "What I was doing has a name - it's called outcome prompting. And I'm betting most of you have done the exact same thing this week. Let me show you what I mean..."
